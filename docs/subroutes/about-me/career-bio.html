FAQ

Art history?

Prior to data science, I was a professional art historian and specialist. (See here for details.)

A common theme across every experience was the importance of communicating abstract concepts to a non-technical audience. This was especially challenging when I was at Christie's, as this communication often involved tens of millions of dollars of fine art. For example, when given a Picasso, I had to research that masterpiece well enough to not only predict its opening price, but also recommend growth strategies to stakeholders in order to maximize their piece's closing price.

In many ways, my art experience was heavily multidisciplinary, as it drew upon both the analytical framework for pricing and the creative, open-ended domain of art history. My job was to put a numerical value on the inherently non-numerical, which taught me many data research methodologies that I continue to use to this day.

Why did you leave?

While the art market was exciting, I gradually realized a significant drawback: its evaluation process was heavily manual--slow and biased. Seeking a more effective approach, I volunteered to explore new tools that could enhance the industry process--an initiative that ultimately led me to discover machine learning for the first time.

I was instantly fascinated by the technology and quickly recognized that if I continued in the art market, I wouldn't have the opportunity to fully develop my technical skills. After graduating from Penn, I made the decision to turn down a return offer to Christie's and instead embarked on a journey of self-teaching in math, computer science, and data science.

What happened after?

Since graduating, I have been deeply committed to self-educating in this domain. I pursued self-paced programs like freeCodeCamp, studied math and computer science through free PDF textbooks, and dedicated myself to numerous passion projects to bridge the gap between theory and practice. While I had to work fulltime to finance this transition, I was ultimately very fortunate to have landed some tutoring and collegiate assistant positions, which helped me maximize the time I spent immersed in data upskilling.

Fast forward to today, I am extremely lucky to have had the opportunities I've had to leverage my data science skills towards making impact! To see examples of my experiences, please refer to industry impact and academia impact.

Math courses while working?

While I was able to self-study math well enough to land positions in the field, having a formal degree in mathematics has always been a personal goal of mine. Coming from a non-STEM background, math just wasn't emphasized in art history, and I didn't want that to be the reason holding me back from progressing further in data science. Thus, I decided to enroll in official university courses alongside my work and research as means of formally building that "traditional" quantitative foundation that normal CS majors have.

With much happiness, I'm proud say that I've almost completed an accredited-equivalency to a bachelors in mathematics! Coursework I have left to take...

AI safety and data science?

While transitioning in data science, I was eager to gain any and all experience. As a result, I rode this momentum into deeper data roles in business intelligence but also in AI research. Through this exposure to academia, I unexpectedly ended up deeply appreciating the user safety aspects of my data products--a feeling that ultimately drove me to pursue dual-careers into both industry (data science) and academia (AI safety/research).

I see this maximizing the net world impact I have through my domain!
